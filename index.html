PhD Research Interest

Designing Proteins
Computationally. Validating Them Experimentally.

"A 1 nM KD, measured by SPR, is the most convincing sentence I can write — because it means a simulation-guided design decision survived contact with reality. My doctoral research will be built around asking when, why, and how reliably that holds true at scale, using both physics-based and learning-based design methods."
01

Molecular Dynamics & Structure Prediction

Using GROMACS and free energy perturbation methods to predict how mutations alter protein structure, stability, and binding energy — validated against a measured 1 nM KD in my thesis work. I have also used AlphaFold/ColabFold for structure prediction, and want to extend into longer-timescale dynamics and conformational ensemble modeling of therapeutic protein-receptor complexes.

02

Computational Protein Design

Applying Osprey for sequence-structure-function-guided mutation design — demonstrated by a quantified, SPR-validated affinity gain in IL-22R1. My doctoral interest is extending from targeted point-mutation design toward generative and deep-learning-based design methods (e.g. RFdiffusion-style backbone design, ESM-guided sequence design) for engineering novel binding interfaces.

03

Structural Bioinformatics

Integrating sequence analysis, structural modeling, and experimental characterization data to identify and validate design targets. I am particularly interested in receptor-ligand interface engineering for autoimmune and inflammatory disease targets, where decoy-receptor and biosimilar strategies have direct clinical and regulatory relevance.

How my background uniquely positions me for PhD research

1

I have already completed a full computational-to-experimental protein design cycle

MSc thesis: designed four mutations in IL-22R1 using PyMOL + GROMACS + Osprey, expressed the mutant in E. coli, characterized it by HPLC and mass spectrometry, and measured a 1 nM KD by SPR — at least 7-fold tighter than literature wild-type affinity. This is not background knowledge — it is a quantified, documented research output.

2

I understand how protein characterization data is used in real-world applications

As a Regulatory Affairs Expert, I write the CMC documentation that describes biologics to health authorities. I know exactly what analytical characterization data — PTM profiles, higher-order structure, binding assays — needs to look like, and why it matters beyond the paper.

3

I am motivated by a specific, current research question

Classical mutation design (Osprey, GROMACS-based FEP) gave me a 1 nM, SPR-validated result — but it required months of targeted, hypothesis-driven work. Deep-learning methods like AlphaFold, ESM, and RFdiffusion promise to make this faster and more generalizable. My doctoral question: how do physics-based and learning-based design methods compare in predicting real, experimentally measurable affinity gains — and where does each approach still fail? This is the question I want to pursue at the doctoral level.

Research Projects

Scientific Work

MSc Thesis · 2021–2024 · Tehran University

In Silico Engineering of IL-22R1 for Enhanced Ligand Affinity in Autoimmune Diseases

Introduced four point mutations in IL-22R1 guided by molecular dynamics and free energy calculations to enhance binding affinity with IL-22. Expressed mutant protein in E. coli; characterized by SDS-PAGE, RP-HPLC, SEC-HPLC, and mass spectrometry including PTM analysis. Measured mutant KD of 1 nM by Surface Plasmon Resonance — at least a 7-fold and up to a 45-fold improvement over literature-reported wild-type IL-22R1 affinity (7–45 nM range). Identified as a candidate decoy receptor for Rheumatoid Arthritis and Psoriasis.
